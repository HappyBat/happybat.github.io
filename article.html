AD(H)D Or Living Life Without Remote Control

In the United Kingdom AD(H)D affects about 3-5% of children and 2% of adults. That means in a class of 20 to 35 kids, chances are at least one of them has AD(H)D. Can you think of 50 adult relatives, friends, colleagues or acquaintances? Chances are, at least one of them will have AD(H)D, too. Chances also are, they do not know they have it. Experts estimate that three quarters of all adults with AD(H)D are undiagnosed. They might struggle in life, not knowing they could seek for help and effective treatment. In fact, people with AD(H)D are over seven times more likely to attempt suicide and ten times more likely to complete it.

But what does it mean for adults to have AD(H)D? Let us imagine a few hypothetic but probable scenarios. Picture the following situation:

Your employee and co-worker Brian has stayed at the office overnight for three days in a row. He hardly ate anything and is not seen by anyone unless they enter his office. In fact, he does not even change his posture. He seems to be so deep into his new project that even the deafening noise of the building site leaves him undisturbed. If spoken to, he barely reacts nor remembers any conversation attempt there might have been. When on the fourth day he stands at your door with a spacey look and bloodshot eyes, the project, which was scheduled to be completed in two months, is finished...

Brian has Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (AD(H)D).

You would not have thought so? No wonder, the term of this disability is heavily misleading. Individuals with AD(H)D do not lack attention, they struggle in regulating it. Hans Biegert, school director of a very successful private school in Germany that has a special teaching approach for pupils with AD(H)D, explains it like this: “AD(H)D is, in its essence, a self-regulatory disorder. It is not concentration but impulse and attention regulation that are disrupted. Positive feedback can help sustaining attention. That is why children with AD(H)D can focus on their Gameboy, PlayStation and Nintendo for hours on end – they constantly generate feedback.” So how about Brian? Does this explain his behaviour?

No. What Brian is experiencing is a phenomenon that has been frequently observed in individuals with AD(H)D, especially

those with more severe symptoms. It is colloquially termed as ‘hyperfocus’. Hyperfocus means to be so intensely focused on something, that one is no longer aware of the outer world or even personal physical needs. This can lead to heightened productiveness, but it can also lead to the neglection of crucial tasks, appointments or meetings. Hyperfocus has been subject of research, despite it not being listed among the diagnostic criteria for AD(H)D. School director Biegert describes one case where one of his pupils, who can barely sit still, joined the fishing club and sits at the river bank for hours.

Unfortunately, people with AD(H)D do not naturally have control over their hyperfocus, as they do not have control over their attention in general. That is why someone with AD(H)D might start a project overly enthusiastically (hyperfocused) but lose interest in it quite soon. It does not help to tell someone with AD(H)D “but you could focus on xy for days so you can as well just focus now”. People with attention regulation problems cannot ‘just focus’, just like someone with one leg cannot ‘just walk’.

You, as Brian’s boss, might feel quite ambivalent towards him: He has periods of extreme productivity and he helps the company advance with his flow of ideas and creativity. But he also rushes into the office ten minutes late every day, he procrastinates projects dangerously long and is unable to prioritise tasks. He forgets deadlines and makes careless mistakes in many projects. At one point, the survival of the company was at stake because he had sent an email containing a corporate secret to the wrong recipient. Should you fire him?

For many people with AD(H)D there is no way they can concentrate on mundane and mechanical tasks unless they take medication. It prevents them from managing their lives and paying bills, but it also interferes with professional life, as they rarely get accommodations in the workplace. Individuals with AD(H)D also have distorted perceptions of time, also called time blindness, that leads to difficulties in time estimation. They also have slower processing speed: the speed in which a person can understand and react to the information they receive.

All this leads to troubles at work and frequent job changes but also to problems in social relationships. This is usually sobering for both parties involved, as one feels unappreciated and neglected and the other gets increasingly desperate with their unsuccessful attempts to change their behaviour. add the special something

It does not come as a surprise that "those with ADHD receive 20,000 more negative messages by age 12 than those without the condition”, according to the AD(H)D expert Dr. Ned Hallowell. “They view themselves as fundamentally different and flawed." There are not many accommodations for someone with AD(H)D in the workplace either. “Autism is quite popular in a way” Cardiff researcher Syed points out, “there are accommodations for it and people understand the issues to a certain degree. AD(H)D, however, is like the poor cousin of autism.” TRUDY The media, Trudy says, does “no longer repeatedly post articles discrediting the existence of conditions such as dyslexia or autism, they need to treat ADHD with the same level of acceptance.”

Now imagine the following situation:

You meet your friend Heather and her son Jonathan at the school yard. Heather tells you that your daughter is the only friend who invited him to a birthday party. One of the teachers interrupts you just then, saying that Jonathan was disturbing class and did not do his homework for the last three days. Suddenly, you hear your child screaming. Jonathan is holding her toy, looking baffled. You begin to wonder, whether Heather has it all under control and if she is providing any structure and guidance at all for her son. Maybe she just places him in front of the TV all day long?

Who could have AD(H)D? Both, Heather and Jonathan. And that is what makes it so hard for Heather. Let us assume Heather and her son are undiagnosed. Heather is setting schedules, alarms and reminders everywhere, for both her son and herself. She takes her son out all day so that he can get rid of his surplus energy. And yet it is not enough. His talkativity and impulsivity leads to problems with peers despite her efforts to teach adequate social behaviour. Jonathan can never concentrate on his homework for more than fifteen minutes. She suspects AD(H)D but is afraid of getting him diagnosed because of all the stigma associated with it. People will just think it is bad parenting. Sadly, Heather’s situation is not that uncommon. Bad parenting or unfortunate environmental conditions are still wrongly perceived as causes for AD(H)D. “A big myth is that people just do not think it [AD(H)D] is real, despite all the scientific evidence. People say it is just made up and an excuse for naughty children” says Sharifah Syed, AD(H)D researcher and psychologist at Cardiff University. She explains such stigma is a reason why it takes parents a while to accept their children have AD(H)D. What many people do not know is that AD(H)D is highly heritable. The heritability is around 80 percent, which means that it runs in many families. In fact, you could say it is as heritable as intelligence. ‘Oh, that’s good news’, you may think, so we just allocate the AD(H)D gene? Sadly not. “Several genes could be identified”, says Professor Klaus-Peter Lesch, who is a highly reputed scientist in the area of neurobiology, “that can indicate AD(H)D and even the conditions that can occur together with it”. However, these genes are different ones for everyone. Only within a few all-AD(H)D families a certain gene could be found. A general single identifier like ‘the AD(H)D gene’, however, is non-existent. As the heritability of AD(H)D is not well known in the public and the disorder is still seen as an excuse by many people, individuals like our mum Heather struggle to refute the stigma. That does not make the struggle less real. “Just spending and hour or two for the assessment with an AD(H)D child can be so exhausting.” Says Cardiff researcher Syed sympathetically. As she describes it, in order to manage and live with a child with AD(H)D 24/7 “you have to be almost like a super mum”. Professor Lesch from Germany, father of four children with AD(H)D agrees with her in that. It is especially difficult if one parent has the disorder themselves. He explains that mothers with AD(H)D have a low tolerance level and children with AD(H)D tend to have the baby colic, often due to allergies, with makes it difficult for the mothers to breastfeed them enough. According to current research, there are more and more indications that a lack of breastfeeding can lead to problems in brain development. Often, Lesch resumes, families break apart because of all the difficulties. If this was not hard enough, one must not forget about the stigma. A recent study concluded that the overwhelming majority of adults with AD(H)D had doubts about the validity of AD(H)D as a mental disorder. They perceived public stigma and discrimination, which caused them to suffer more psychological distress, self-esteem issues, functional impairment and decreased quality of life. In general, it made their ‘burden of disease’ much heavier. “The attitude towards AD(H)D is still not very good and a lot of stigma is attached to it in the media.” says researcher Syed. People with AD(H)D often get to hear phrases like “Oh, I also get distracted sometimes” or “ Yeah, I’m like that, always forgetting things…”, which can feel quite belittling for someone who is seriously affected by these dispositions. “All of us have some of it to some degree but for someone with AD(H)D it is like that ALL the time, 24/7. It causes disruptions in their everyday life, from impeding them to focus, to hindering them to manage their relationships and their money.” says Syed. That is the difference. Now, imagine our final example:

You are Lucy, a 12-year-old girl. Teachers get mad at you for daydreaming in lectures and missing nearly everything during the classes. Somehow though, you manage to keep up and do well in school. However, doing homework takes you all day. You are quiet and introvert and often afraid of other people, which might be due to your low self-esteem. Still, you have a few good friends and are generally liked by classmates. Eight years later, you find yourself at university. Suddenly, you are required to organise yourself. No one checks the homework, gives you constant feedback or does the laundry for you. Your room gets messy, no matter how hard you try to prevent it. You are too ashamed to allow people for visit. Often you are so late for lectures, that you decide not to go at all. If you manage to, you cannot ignore all the noise around you. Your brain shuts down. You forget appointments. You work several nights in a row before every deadline, given you realised there was a deadline. Your grades drop. You know you are far away from reaching up to your potential. One day, you cannot stand up from bed anymore. And the next day. And the next. After two weeks you go the doctor, asking to get diagnosed for depression.

“Adults with AD(H)D seldomly come to see a doctor or psychiatrist because of their core symptoms, but they come for disorders like substance abuse, depression or anxiety disorders, that derived from the AD(H)D in the first place” says Professor Lesch. “That is shutting the stable door after the horse has already bolted.” In fact, the discovery that many patients on the substance abuse station actually had AD(H)D as the primary condition, led Professor Lesch to research on the disorder. “AD(H)D is clearly undiagnosed, especially in girls and women” says Lesch. They often internalise symptoms and are good at fitting into the social expectations. Moreover, they tend to not show hyperactive symptoms (they have ADD instead of ADHD). There would be at least twice as many patients with AD(H)D if everyone were diagnosed, he says. TRUDY “The view of ADHD is predominantly male and negative.” “I did not get diagnosed when I needed it, because I was, according to my GP ‘too academic, too bright and not disruptive enough.” The main problems in adults, Lesch says, is that they never reach their potential and there is a huge discrepancy between what they could and expect themselves to achieve and what they are actually achieve. Now imagine yourself, undiagnosed Lucy, in the same situation but under Corona virus conditions. The last bit of your structure, your routines: gone. No social interaction, only remote learning. TRUDY At first, it might not seem straightforward that people with AD(H)D should suffer disproportionally more from the COVID-19 pandemic than neurotypicals. COVID-19 has led to an increase in many mental health conditions across the globe. Anxiety, depression and sleeping disorders among other conditions, are soaring due to this pandemic. Now fate would have it so, that these conditions are naturally all very prevalent among people with AD(H)D. One study conducted in China comes to the result that “during the COVID-19 outbreak, children’s ADHD behaviours significantly worsened in comparison to their normal state.” They ascribe it to the “loss of daily routine and the lack of interpersonal and social interaction.” While for most countries there is no information on the availability of health services during the pandemic, in Ireland it had, as a study suggests, a “significant impact on the provision of mental health services for children and adolescents with ADHD.” To sum up, people with AD(H)D are highly impacted by these restrictions and might have big difficulties accessing help. So, what did we learn from Brian, Heather, Jonathan and Lucy? AD(H)D is a highly heritable attention regulation impediment that can be a quite debilitating factor in people’s lives. Most of the time, it remains unseen. It rarely occurs alone but together with other disorders and there is a lot of stigma attached to it. Is it all bad though? No, only people do not know that! “The understanding and empathy for AD(H)D in the population is very limited, but so is the knowledge regarding the positive resources of it: creativity, the ability to think “outside of the box” and enthusiasm” says Professor Lesch. “If I ask you to name me 10 successful entrepreneurs” says Biegert “the majority of those probably have AD(H)D”. “They overcome boundaries and create new opportunities. They have the courage to try things others would shy away from.” “It is important not to disqualify it as weakness of character” says Lesch. Also, there is effective therapy and treatment. School director Biegert, on the other hand, stresses the importance of positive feedback, as it is something, people with AD(H)D rarely get to hear. For the future, researcher Syed wishes for more support for people with AD(H)D: “For adults it is especially stigmatising to go to the psychiatrist and seek help. If there was more support, people will be more likely to seek help.” FOCUS is part of my final degree project in Computational Journalism. The whole project is composed by the game, an article and the dissertation per se. My project aims to help the public develop a greater understanding of AD(H)D.
